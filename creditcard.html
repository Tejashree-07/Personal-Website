CREDIT CARD FRAUD PREDICTION

As part of a data-centric elective in my graduate coursework, I worked on a team project aimed at detecting credit card fraud using a real-world transactional dataset of over 550,000 entries.

The project involved cleaning and engineering features from the raw data, such as user demographics, transaction timing, and behavioral patterns. Feature transformations and time-based analytics were implemented to enhance model inputs. A range of models—including logistic regression, Naive Bayes, SVM, LDA, Random Forest, and XGBoost were trained and evaluated. These models were assessed based on precision-recall balance, ROC-AUC, and F1-score.

XGBoost achieved the best performance with an AUC of 0.998 and F1-score of 0.98. Feature importance analysis revealed transaction amount, category, and fraud frequency as the most predictive features. Emphasis was placed on evaluation metrics like ROC-AUC and threshold tuning to optimize detection accuracy. The workflow demonstrated strong applicability of machine learning to classification tasks in imbalanced data environments—relevant to process optimization, fault detection, and risk analytics in engineering domains.
