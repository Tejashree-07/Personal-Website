An Integrated Experimental and Simulation Framework for the Design of High Thermal Conductivity Anisotropic Thermal Interface Materials

As part of my Master’s program, I undertook a directed research under the guidance of Dr. Yong Chen. My research addresses the growing demand for efficient thermal management in compact and high-performance semiconductor devices. The focus is on developing micro-scale thermal management solutions to optimize heat dissipation in semiconductor chips using additive manufacturing techniques.

As chips get smaller and faster, managing heat becomes mission-critical—especially in CPUs, GPUs, and 3D-stacked ICs. In this research, I designed an advanced thermal interface material (TIM) tailored for modern semiconductor packaging. The proposed composite blends thermoplastic polyurethane (TPU), boron nitride platelets, and nickel-coated carbon fibers—creating a directional thermal pathway to tackle localized hotspots more efficiently

Using Direct Ink Writing (DIW) and magnetic alignment as core principles, I developed a novel dual-alignment strategy to orient fillers both horizontally and vertically—without needing costly, large-scale magnets. Though the material wasn't fabricated, I built and validated a full simulation framework using Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) and Design of Experiments (DOE).

Key Results: I established a quantitative relationship between inlet pressure and shear stress inside the DIW nozzle—a first in this context. Shear stress increased from ~220 Pa to ~600 Pa as pressure rose from 10 to 14 psi. Velocity ranged from 3.4 mm/s to 10.7 m/s, indicating the importance of balancing flow rate with alignment quality. An optimal configuration of 250 µm nozzle diameter at 8.35 psi was identified, providing high shear for alignment with stable flow—offering valuable guidance for future fabrication.

I believe that bridging simulation with fabrication can unlock the next generation of thermal materials for semiconductor systems. Let’s innovate together.
